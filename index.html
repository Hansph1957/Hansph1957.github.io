Maximum Rectangle in an Ellipse

A Simple and Elegant Geometric Construction

Introduction

The classical problem of finding the largest rectangle that can be inscribed in an ellipse is usually solved using calculus or affine transformations. This page presents a purely geometric construction that produces the maximal rectangle in a visually intuitive and elegant way.

The Ellipse and Its Bounding Rectangle

Consider the ellipse defined by:

(x²)/(a²) + (y²)/(b²) = 1

The smallest axis-aligned rectangle that contains the ellipse has corners at (±a, ±b). This rectangle will play a key role in the construction.

The Diagonal Construction

Draw both diagonals of the bounding rectangle. These diagonals intersect the ellipse in exactly four points. Remarkably, these four points form the vertices of the largest possible axis-aligned rectangle inside the ellipse.

The intersection points occur at:

(±a/√2, ±b/√2)

Analytic Verification

Substituting the diagonal line y = (b/a)x into the ellipse equation gives:

2x²/a² = 1 → x = ±a/√2 → y = ±b/√2

These four points form a rectangle of width a√2 and height b√2, giving area:

A = 2ab

Conclusion

This geometric construction provides a simple and elegant way to identify the largest axis-aligned rectangle that can be inscribed in an ellipse. It avoids calculus entirely and offers a visually intuitive method suitable for teaching, presentations, and mathematical outreach.
